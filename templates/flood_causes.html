Causes of Floods

A look at common factors

Floods are one of the most common natural disasters in the world. In fact, they are an increasing threat to our lives, in terms of frequency and severity. Here are some reasons for the occurrence of floods.

Heavy Rainfall

Heavy rain, especially within a short period of time, is the top cause of flooding. When there is intense rainfall, drainage systems, both natural and man-made, are not able to drain water as quickly as it needs to, thus causing floods. In recent times, climate change has brought even heavier rainfall, increasing the chances and severity of flooding.

Proximity to Rivers

Rivers and watercourses run the risk of flooding during rainfall, thus people living in their proximity get subjected to floods, even without heavy rain. Increasingly heavier rainfall over time only increases the likelihood of overflowing rivers

Urbanisation

Cities are largely made of concrete and other impermeable materials. This prevents water from draining into the ground, which is naturally porous. When drainage systems reach their capacity, water collects on the surface and causes flooding.

Vegetation cover

Vegetations are good at intercepting water. When it rains, plants intercept a lot of water through leaves and branches, slowing the rate at which water hits the ground. Roots dig deep into the ground and create spaces for water to drain into. Thus, in areas with poor vegetation cover, like cities, there is a high chance of flooding.

These causes are not isolated, and there can be multiple reasons applied at the same time to why an area floods. It will be difficult to displace increasingly large amounts of water and there is only so much man-made drainage systems can handle. Thus, greater effort needs to be put in to reduce climate change, and to integrate more flora into our concrete environment.
